## Overview

Email Reputation returns a layered view of every email address: deliverability status, quality signals, sender identity, domain intelligence, risk flags, and breach history. The three patterns below cover the most common ways teams use those signals in production.

---

## 1. Deliverability verification at point of entry

Validate addresses when you collect them (at signup forms, lead import, or checkout) before they enter your database or email queue.

A bounce rate above 2% hurts your sender reputation. A list padded with undeliverable addresses inflates your CRM and ESP costs. A signup form that accepts anything is an open door for throwaway accounts.

### Fields to use

| Field | Signal | Tier |
|---|---|---|
| `email_deliverability.status` | Primary pass/fail gate: `deliverable`, `undeliverable`, `unknown` | Starter+ |
| `email_deliverability.status_detail` | Explains the reason behind the status. Check this before blocking an `undeliverable` result: `mailbox_full` means the address is real but the inbox is temporarily over capacity | Starter+ |
| `email_deliverability.is_format_valid` | Catches typos before you hit the network | Starter+ |
| `email_deliverability.is_smtp_valid` | Confirms the mailbox actually exists | Starter+ |
| `email_deliverability.is_mx_valid` | Confirms the domain accepts mail | Starter+ |
| `email_quality.is_disposable` | Flags throwaway addresses from Mailinator, Yopmail, etc. | Professional |

### The pattern

Call the API at the moment of entry. Before acting on an `undeliverable` result, check `status_detail`: a `mailbox_full` value means the address is real; the inbox is temporarily over capacity. Treat that as a soft failure, not a permanent block: allow the user through, or queue for retry. For all other `undeliverable` results, block. Block on `is_disposable` as well. Pass through on `deliverable` and `unknown`, always failing open when the result is ambiguous. Return a clear error when you block so the user can correct their address: "That email address doesn't look right. Please check for typos."

## 2. Lead scoring and routing

Not every email address that passes deliverability validation is equally valuable. `is_free_email`, quality scores, and domain intelligence let you prioritize leads before they reach your sales team.

A marketing lead at `sarah@gmail.com` and one at `sarah@acmecorp.com` both have deliverable addresses. But they're different buyers. Routing them the same way means your sales team wastes time on low-intent consumer signups, and high-value enterprise leads wait longer than they should.

### Fields to use

| Field | Signal | Tier |
|---|---|---|
| `email_sender.organization_name` | Company name tied to the email address | Standard+ |
| `email_sender.organization_type` | Distinguishes company accounts from individual accounts | Standard+ |
| `email_domain.domain_age` | Newer domains carry more risk; established domains signal legitimacy | Standard+ |
| `email_quality.is_free_email` | `true` for Gmail, Yahoo, and consumer providers (the primary B2B/consumer split) | Professional |
| `email_quality.score` | 0.01–0.99 confidence score for overall email quality | Professional |
| `email_quality.is_role` | `true` for `info@`, `sales@`, `support@`; often lower intent | Professional |

### The pattern

At signup, set `is_free_email` as the first routing fork. Business email addresses go to your sales-qualified pipeline. Consumer addresses go to self-serve onboarding. From there, use `email_quality.score` and domain intelligence to further prioritize: high-scoring business addresses from established domains get fast-tracked; low-scoring addresses or very new domains get flagged for review.

## 3. Fraud detection and risk assessment

Fraud doesn't always look like fraud at the format level. Disposable domains, compromised accounts, spam trap patterns, and high-risk email providers all pass basic validation. Catching them requires signals that go beyond deliverability.

The fields below go beyond format and deliverability to surface the signals that most fraud patterns leave behind. They require Professional; the Free tier returns them all for testing.

### Fields to use

| Field | Signal | Tier |
|---|---|---|
| `email_quality.is_disposable` | Throwaway addresses from known disposable providers | Professional |
| `email_quality.is_username_suspicious` | Auto-generated or randomized usernames | Professional |
| `email_risk.address_risk_status` | Risk level of the address: `low`, `medium`, `high` | Professional |
| `email_risk.domain_risk_status` | Risk level of the domain: `low`, `medium`, `high` | Professional |
| `email_quality.is_dmarc_enforced` | Domain enforces DMARC; signals a legitimate, established sending setup | Professional |
| `email_quality.minimum_age` | Estimated age of the address in days; very new addresses carry more risk | Professional |
| `email_breaches.total_breaches` | Number of known data breaches involving this email | Professional |
| `email_breaches.breached_domains` | List of breach sources, useful for assessing account takeover risk | Professional |

### The pattern

Build a risk scoring function that aggregates these signals into a single score or tier. A `high` address risk status, combined with a suspicious username and zero days of minimum age, is a strong fraud signal even if the address is technically deliverable. For high-value transactions (account creation with payment, enterprise trial activation), use breach data to flag accounts that have appeared in known credential dumps.
